Every iconic original and its honest field of homages, ranked by fidelity to the published rubric. Pick a watch to see prices, movements and where to buy.

The database currently covers 24 originals and 60 homages, every one a real, currently-sold product. Street prices run from $45 to $560 with a median around $185 — 23 of the 60 come in under $150, and only three cross $400. The overwhelming majority are proper automatics (50 of 60, mostly Seiko NH35/NH34-class or Swiss Sellita/ETA), with a handful of meca-quartz chronographs and hand-wound mechanicals where that's what the segment actually sells.

Fidelity scores range from 55 to 92. The scale is deliberately hard to max out: a score in the 80s means the watch reads unmistakably like its inspiration on the wrist, and only one watch in the database — Steinhart's Ocean One 39, against the Submariner — clears 90. The score measures design closeness only; it says nothing about build quality, and it is never a claim that anything is a copy. Houses that show up most: San Martin (20 entries), Pagani Design (10), Steeldive and Baltany (6 each). Roughly half the field (27 of 60) is carried on Amazon; where a house sells cheaper direct, the entry says so.

Common questions

What counts as a homage, and what doesn't? A homage is a watch openly sold under its own brand that borrows the design language of an icon — the case shape, the bezel, the dial furniture. It is not a replica: nothing in this database carries another brand's name, logo or reference, and we never list counterfeit product. The line matters legally and morally, and it's why houses like Steinhart, San Martin and Pagani Design can sell these watches in the open.

How are the fidelity scores set? Five weighted checks from the published rubric: dial and handset, bezel, case shape and proportions, movement class, and spec parity (size, water resistance, crystal). Each homage is scored against its specific original, not against an abstract ideal — which is why a $95 watch can outscore a $500 one when it simply looks closer.

Why do most entries cost $100–$400 when the originals cost five figures? Because that's where the homage market actually lives. The median original in the database lists around ten thousand dollars; the median homage is $185. The gap pays for the crown on the dial, the in-house movement and the waiting list — not, for the most part, for timekeeping. A Seiko NH35 or Sellita SW200 in a well-machined case keeps time within seconds a day.

Which type should a first-time buyer start with? Dive watches are the deepest field here (the Submariner alone has six ranked homages, from $95 to $520) and the most forgiving to wear. If a dressier profile fits better, the Tank and Santos fields cover quartz and automatic options under $300. Every original page ranks its field by fidelity, so the shortlist is already made.
